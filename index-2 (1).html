“ I don't just want to use AI — I want to understand it deeply enough to build with it responsibly.

I'm a Computer Science Engineering student with a deep interest in Artificial Intelligence, Generative AI, and Large Language Models. My work sits at the intersection of solid computer science fundamentals — data structures, system design, databases — and applied AI, where I build things like retrieval-augmented generation pipelines, computer-vision-based health tools, and AI agents.

Alongside AI/ML, I'm equally comfortable across the full stack — designing REST APIs with FastAPI and Flask, building interfaces in React, and modeling data in SQL, PostgreSQL and MongoDB. I care about shipping systems that are not just accurate, but usable, fast and well-engineered end to end.

My goal is to grow into an AI Engineer role at a product-based technology company, where I can work on systems used at scale and keep learning at the edge of what generative AI can do.

My journey so far

My interest in computer science started the way it does for a lot of engineers — with curiosity about how the software on my screen actually worked underneath. That curiosity turned into a habit of building: small C and Java programs first, then web pages, then full applications with a backend and a database behind them. Somewhere along the way I got pulled into machine learning, and once I trained my first model and watched it make a correct prediction, I was hooked. That moment is a big part of why I chose to specialize in AI/ML and Generative AI rather than stop at "just" full stack development.

Over the course of my B.Tech, I've tried to treat every course and every project as a chance to go one level deeper — not just to pass an exam, but to actually understand the "why" behind data structures, database design, operating systems and networks. That fundamentals-first approach is what let me pick up Generative AI concepts like RAG, embeddings and agentic workflows quickly once I started exploring them on my own, well beyond what any single course covered. Maintaining a 9.21 CGPA throughout has mattered to me, but it's always been a side effect of that curiosity rather than the goal itself.

The AI/ML virtual internship with Infosys Springboard gave me my first structured exposure to real-world ML workflows — data cleaning, model evaluation, and the kind of iterative debugging that textbooks rarely prepare you for. It confirmed something I'd already started to suspect: I enjoy the applied side of AI far more than the purely theoretical side. I want to build things people can actually open in a browser and use, not just notebooks that produce a metric.

How I like to work

I tend to start any new project by writing down the actual problem in plain language before I open an editor — it's easy to jump straight to "which library should I use" and lose sight of who the system is actually for. From there I like to build a rough end-to-end version early, even if it's unpolished, so I can see the whole pipeline working before I spend time refining any one piece. That habit came from a few early projects where I over-engineered a single component and only later realized the overall flow didn't hold together.

I'm comfortable working independently — most of my AI/ML and full stack projects have been self-driven, which means I've had to be my own reviewer, tester and documentation writer. That's taught me to be honest about a project's weak points rather than just its demo-ready parts, and it's part of why I care about things like clean READMEs, sensible commit history and code that a reviewer at a product company could actually read.

Beyond the code

Outside of coursework and projects, I spend a lot of time reading about new AI research and product launches — trying to understand not just what a new model or technique can do, but why it was built that way. I'm an active problem solver on LeetCode, partly for interview preparation and partly because I genuinely enjoy the puzzle of finding a cleaner algorithm. I also enjoy explaining technical concepts to classmates and juniors; teaching something is usually the fastest way I've found to discover the gaps in my own understanding.

Where I'm headed

My near-term goal is to land an internship or entry-level role at a product-based technology company — somewhere like Google, Microsoft, NVIDIA, Apple, Ericsson or Cisco — where I can work on AI-driven products used by real people at real scale, alongside engineers I can learn from. Longer term, I want to grow into a role where I'm designing the systems, not just implementing them: someone who understands both the machine learning underneath a product and the full stack required to ship it responsibly and reliably.
